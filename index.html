Every major event in the Audi–Sauber deal from 2021 to 2026 debut.

Late 2021

Andretti rejected, door opens for Audi

Sauber owner Finn Rausing (via Islero Investments / Longbow Finance) rejects a ~€350M offer from Michael Andretti to buy the team. Rausing demands job continuity at Hinwil and an extra €250M commitment — too much for Andretti. Audi, already in exploratory talks with multiple teams, moves to the front of the queue.

Background

Spring–Summer 2022

Audi evaluates three potential partners

Audi holds serious discussions with McLaren, Williams (via former VW WRC boss Jost Capito), and Sauber. McLaren talks collapse — reportedly too expensive and complex. Williams talks go nowhere. Sauber emerges as the favourite: a state-of-the-art wind tunnel Audi already knew well from WEC/LMP testing, clean ownership structure, manageable price.

Pre-deal

26 August 2022

Audi officially announces F1 entry

At the Belgian Grand Prix in Spa, Audi CEO Markus Duesmann announces the brand will enter Formula 1 in 2026 as a power unit manufacturer. No team partner named yet. Key stated rationale: 2026 regulation reset (50% electric power, sustainable fuels) aligns with Audi's EV transition strategy. VW Group's supervisory board signs off.

Announcement

26 October 2022

Sauber confirmed as works partner

Audi and Sauber jointly announce the partnership. Sauber will become the Audi factory team from 2026, running Audi power units. The Alfa Romeo naming arrangement (title sponsorship since 2018) will end after 2023. Sauber's 16-year Ferrari engine supply deal will end after 2025. Finn Rausing: Audi are the "best strategic partner." Fred Vasseur (Sauber TP at the time) calls it the "best option for the future."

Partnership signed

December 2022

Andreas Seidl appointed CEO of Sauber

Audi poaches Andreas Seidl from McLaren — where he had been Team Principal since 2019, overseeing McLaren's return to competitiveness. Seidl is formally hired by Sauber, but it is widely understood he was recruited specifically to lead the Audi F1 project from day one. Simultaneously, Fred Vasseur departs Sauber for Ferrari (replacing Mattia Binotto as Ferrari TP).

Leadership

January 2023

Phase 1: Audi acquires minority stake (~25%)

Audi completes its first equity stake in Sauber Group. The exact percentage is not disclosed publicly but is widely reported as approximately 25%. The original deal structure: Audi would scale up incrementally — 25% in 2023, ~50% in 2024, ~75% in 2025 — with Finn Rausing (Islero Investments) retaining 25% indefinitely. Julius Seebach joins the Sauber board as Audi's official representative. Power unit development ramps up in Neuburg, Germany.

Stake acquired

2024 season (all year)

Sauber crisis: 0 points all season

The C44 (branded as Kick Sauber) is the worst car on the grid. Valtteri Bottas and Zhou Guanyu score zero championship points across the entire season — the first time in years a team has been entirely pointless. Internal reports suggest the team is entirely focused on 2026 car development rather than fixing 2024. Binotto later says: "When I came in there was not only zero points, but really even no plans nor developments... Everything was only focused on 2026, but that was for me somehow a problem." Leadership instability accelerates.

Performance crisis

8 March 2024

Phase 2: Audi announces 100% takeover

Audi's supervisory board (and VW Group's board) vote to abandon the incremental stake structure and move immediately to full ownership. The stated reason: "accelerating preparations for 2026." The real driver: Seidl had been pushing for Audi to do more, sooner — and Sauber's underperformance made the case. The deal had been agreed with Finn Rausing's Islero Investments. Andreas Seidl formally named Audi F1 Team CEO. Oliver Hoffmann steps down from Audi AG board to focus exclusively on the F1 project (later departs entirely).

100% takeover announced

1 August 2024

Mattia Binotto joins as COO/CTO

Binotto — former Ferrari Team Principal (2019–2022) — joins Sauber as Chief Operating Officer and Chief Technical Officer. His appointment is widely seen as a direct replacement for the Seidl-led structure. Seidl is effectively marginalised and departs within months. Alessandro Alunni Bravi remains as on-track Team Representative while the new leadership structure takes shape.

Leadership overhaul

November 2024

QIA acquires ~30% minority stake

The Qatar Investment Authority — the sovereign wealth fund that also owns Paris Saint-Germain FC and holds 17% of VW Group — announces a "substantial minority" investment in Sauber Holding AG. Reported value: ~$360M for ~30% of the company. This implies a total team valuation of approximately $1.2 billion. The QIA deal is announced at the Qatar Grand Prix, giving it maximum visibility. Audi retains 70% ownership and full operational control.

QIA investment

January 2025

100% takeover legally completed

Audi confirms the full acquisition of Sauber Holding AG is complete. Total price: ~€600M (~$650M). Sauber Motorsport AG, Sauber Holding AG and all subsidiaries now fully owned by Audi AG. QIA holds ~30% (acquired separately from Audi post-completion). Jonathan Wheatley's appointment as Team Principal is confirmed — he was to join in summer 2025 after gardening leave from Red Bull, but is released early. Alessandro Alunni Bravi departs.

Deal closes

2025 season (first half)

Disastrous start — then remarkable recovery

Stake F1 Team Kick Sauber begins the 2025 season with the same fundamental car problems. Hulkenberg and Bortoleto score 0 points in the first 8 races. Criticism of the team's trajectory intensifies. Binotto is brutally honest internally. Then the new-spec package arrives and the car transforms. The team scores in 6 of the last 8 pre-summer races. Hulkenberg takes Sauber's first podium in decades at Silverstone 2025. Total 2025 season: 70 points, mid-field finish.

Recovery

1 April 2025

Wheatley joins early as Team Principal

Jonathan Wheatley — Red Bull's legendary Sporting Director for 18 years, the man who ran the pit wall for four consecutive Vettel championships and one Verstappen era — joins Sauber months ahead of schedule. Binotto negotiated his early release directly with Red Bull. James Key (ex-McLaren Technical Director) is also in place as TD. The Binotto–Wheatley–Key troika is now complete.

Leadership complete

July 2025

Bicester Technology Centre opens

Sauber opens a new technology office in Bicester Motion, Oxfordshire — in the heart of "Motorsport Valley," the cluster of F1 engineering companies between Oxford and Silverstone. The facility is designed to tap into specialist talent that won't relocate to Switzerland. Third site alongside Hinwil (chassis) and Neuburg (power unit).

Infrastructure

Mid-2025

Revolut named title sponsor

European fintech giant Revolut signs a multi-year title partnership deal with the future Audi F1 team. The team will race as "Audi Revolut F1 Team" from 2026, with the car designation being "R26." Revolut also integrates into the team's financial operations and fan engagement platforms.

Commercial

January 2026

Official Audi F1 Team launch

The team launches as the Audi Revolut F1 Team. Car revealed: the R26. Drivers: Nico Hulkenberg and Gabriel Bortoleto. Power unit: AFR 26 hybrid (V6 1.6L turbo + MGU-K, developed in Neuburg). Pre-season testing in Barcelona and Bahrain impresses — 357 laps in Bahrain test week. Bortoleto goes 10th fastest, Hulkenberg 12th, already closing on midfield pace.

Launch

March 2026

Debut: Australian Grand Prix

Audi races in F1 for the first time. Bortoleto qualifies 10th, Hulkenberg 11th. Bortoleto finishes 9th, scoring Audi's first ever F1 championship point. Hulkenberg hits a technical issue and cannot start. Wheatley departs as Team Principal in March 2026 — Binotto formally takes the TP role.

F1 debut

The five strategic reasons Audi entered F1

1. The 2026 regulation reset — a once-in-a-generation window

F1 introduced entirely new power unit rules in 2026: ~50% electric power (up from ~20%), deletion of the complex MGU-H component, and 100% sustainable fuels. This eliminated Mercedes', Ferrari's and Honda's decade-long engine advantage — every manufacturer starts roughly equal. Audi CEO Duesmann explicitly cited this: "I think it's perfect timing due to the new rules." For a new entrant, this is the only realistic moment to join without being 10 years behind on engine know-how.

2. EV strategy alignment — F1 as a technology lab

Audi is transitioning to become a full EV manufacturer. The 2026 F1 power unit — nearly half electric — is directly relevant to Audi's road car programme. Audi's own words: "Formula 1 serves as a technology driver for both electric mobility and sustainable e-fuels — both topics highly relevant for production models." The MGU-K energy recovery system, battery management, and power deployment software all have road car parallels.

3. Brand transformation — reach younger, global audiences

F1's audience has been transformed by Drive to Survive — US viewership tripled, female fanbase grew significantly, median fan age dropped. Audi has already invested in MLS (soccer), esports, and fashion collabs to reach younger demographics. F1 is the ultimate convergence platform. CCO Massimo Frascella: "We want to shape a brand capable of making a deep emotional connection... that inspires a global community." No other motorsport delivers that scale.

4. Budget cap makes F1 financially viable for the first time

Before the 2021 cost cap (~$135M/year operational cap), F1 was a bottomless money pit. Mercedes spent $400M+/year during their dominant era. The cap fundamentally changed the ROI calculation. Audi CEO Duesmann directly cited the cost cap as a key factor: it "makes it very attractive for us to enter now." A structured investment with capped upside risk is very different from the open-ended spend of the pre-2021 era.

5. Motorsport DNA — the brand's identity is racing

Audi's brand is built on motorsport. 13 Le Mans victories. DTM dominance. Dakar Rally. Formula E championship. The four-ring logo itself traces to Auto Union, which contested Grand Prix racing against Mercedes in the 1930s. F1 is the only major international series Audi has never won. For a brand that defines itself as "Vorsprung durch Technik" (advancement through technology), absence from the pinnacle of the sport is a gap in the story.

Why Sauber — not McLaren, Williams or a new team?

McLaren talks failedAudi held early discussions with McLaren but the deal collapsed — reportedly too expensive, too complicated ownership structure (Bahraini sovereign fund Mumtalakat holds a stake), and McLaren was unwilling to become a pure works team for Audi.
Williams went nowhereTalks with Williams (via former VW WRC boss Jost Capito) also ended without a deal. Williams had recently been acquired by Dorilton Capital and wasn't available on terms Audi wanted.
Starting from scratch ruled outBuilding a new F1 team from zero takes 5–7 years and requires FOM approval (notoriously difficult — Andretti spent 3 years fighting for a slot). Acquiring an existing team with a valid Concorde Agreement is the only fast path.
Sauber's specific assetsState-of-the-art wind tunnel in Hinwil (one of the best in F1, and Audi already rented it for WEC testing). Valid FOM Concorde Agreement. Clean, acquirable ownership via Islero Investments. Willingness to become a pure works team. Finn Rausing insisted on job protection — which Audi could guarantee.
Price was favourable€600M total acquisition cost vs. competing teams valued at $1B–$6B. The deal was struck before F1 valuations fully inflated. QIA later paid $360M for just 30% — implying a $1.2B valuation by late 2024. Audi effectively bought in before the market appreciated.

Seller side

Finn Rausing

Majority seller / previous owner

Swedish billionaire heir to the Tetra Pak packaging empire. Controls Islero Investments and Longbow Finance S.A. Purchased Sauber in 2016 from Peter Sauber for a reported sum that stabilised the team. Had previously rejected Andretti's €350M offer. Struck final deal with Audi for full exit via Islero Investments.

Exited Jan 2025

Peter Sauber

Founder (legacy)

Founded PP Sauber AG in 1970 in his parents' garage. Built it from Swiss sports car racing to F1 in 1993. Sold controlling stake to Longbow Finance in 2016 after years of financial struggle. All Sauber cars (except BMW-era) named with the letter "C" — honouring his wife Christiane. No ownership role in current entity.

Historical

Islero Investments

Rausing's holding vehicle

The investment entity through which Finn Rausing held his Sauber shares. Named after the Lamborghini Islero. Originally the majority shareholder in Sauber Holding AG. Transferred all shares to Audi AG as part of the full acquisition. Effectively dissolved its Sauber position at close in January 2025.

Exited

Longbow Finance S.A.

Swiss holding company

The Swiss-based investment firm through which the Rausing family (and reportedly Stefan Persson and Karl-Johan Persson of H&M) originally acquired Sauber in 2016. Later restructured so Islero Investments was the direct shareholder vehicle for the Audi deal. Both entities functionally represent Finn Rausing's interests.

Historical structure

Buyer side

Audi AG

Acquiring entity / 70% owner

Premium automotive brand headquartered in Ingolstadt, Bavaria. Subsidiary of Volkswagen Group AG. Revenue ~€69B. The acquiring entity in all phases of the deal. Owns 70% of Sauber Holding AG post-QIA investment. Develops the F1 power unit through its own subsidiary AFR GmbH in Neuburg.

70% owner

Volkswagen Group AG

Ultimate parent / board approval

Audi's parent company. The VW Group supervisory board had to formally approve both the initial partnership (2022), the 100% takeover decision (March 2024), and the QIA deal (Nov 2024). VW Group's financial position and internal politics (including a simultaneous Porsche F1 deal that fell through with Red Bull in 2022) shaped the entire project's pace.

Parent company

Qatar Investment Authority (QIA)

Financial partner / ~30% minority

Qatar's sovereign wealth fund. Manages ~$500B in assets. Also owns PSG football club and holds 17% of VW Group. Acquired ~30% of Sauber Holding AG in November 2024 for ~$360M. Does not take operational control — purely financial. Its involvement serves multiple purposes: fresh capital injection, protection against VW Group budget cuts, and a geopolitical F1 footprint for Qatar.

~30% minority

Audi Formula Racing GmbH (AFR)

Power unit subsidiary

A new company specifically founded by Audi for the F1 power unit programme. Based in Neuburg an der Donau, Bavaria — near Audi's main HQ. Has been developing the AFR 26 hybrid power unit (V6 1.6L turbo + MGU-K) since spring 2022. By December 2024, the complete powertrain ran for the first time in a full race simulation on the test bench. Stefan Dreyer is CTO of AFR.

Audi subsidiary

The acquired entities

Sauber Holding AG

Parent holding company

The Swiss holding company that owns all operating Sauber entities. This is the legal entity that Audi AG acquired 100% of. Registered in Switzerland. After completion in January 2025, Sauber Holding AG is wholly owned by Audi AG (70%) and QIA (30%).

Now Audi-owned

Sauber Motorsport AG

F1 constructor entity

The operating motorsport company and registered F1 constructor. Holds the FOM Concorde Agreement (the licence to race in F1). Based in Hinwil, canton of Zurich. Responsible for chassis design, manufacturing, and race operations. From 2026, operates as the Audi Revolut F1 Team, officially as "Audi Motorsport AG."

Key individuals

Mattia Binotto

Team Principal (from March 2026)

Italian. Former Ferrari Technical Director and Team Principal (resigned end 2022 after difficult season). Joined Sauber 1 Aug 2024 as COO/CTO. Effectively the project head from day one — overseeing both chassis and power unit integration. Took formal TP title after Wheatley's March 2026 departure.

Jonathan Wheatley

Team Principal (2025 – Mar 2026)

British. Red Bull's Sporting Director for 18 years — the pit wall brain behind Vettel's 4 titles and Verstappen's run. Left Red Bull end-2024. Joined Sauber April 2025 (early release negotiated by Binotto). Departed March 2026 for undisclosed reasons. The fastest sporting director hire in F1 history in terms of prestige.

James Key

Technical Director

British. Former McLaren Technical Director (sacked 2023 after poor car performance). Joined Sauber/Audi in 2023. Responsible for car design and aerodynamic development. Has extensive experience across Toro Rosso, Sauber (earlier stint), and McLaren.

Andreas Seidl

CEO (Jan 2023 – mid 2024)

German. Former Porsche LMP1 chief and McLaren Team Principal. Was the key architect of the Audi–Sauber structure. Recruited to run the whole project but was effectively marginalised after Binotto's arrival in August 2024 and departed. His departure was a significant internal story — he had been the public face of the deal.

2

January 2023 — Phase 1 equity (~25% stake)

First formal equity transfer; Rausing retains majority

What transferredAudi acquires approximately 25% of Sauber Group. Exact price not disclosed. Julius Seebach joins Sauber board as Audi's official representative. Sauber retains its name for 2023–2025 while racing under Stake/Kick Sauber branding.
Original planIncremental: 25% (2023) → ~50% (2024) → 75% (2025), with Rausing retaining 25% permanently. This would have limited Audi's upfront financial exposure and given them time to assess the team.
Why it changedSauber's 2024 performance collapse (0 points) made it clear the team needed decisive investment and restructuring that a minority shareholder couldn't force. Seidl pushed hard for Audi to move faster. The incremental model was too slow given the 2026 launch date.

March 2024 announced → January 2025 closed — 100% acquisition

Full takeover of Sauber Holding AG

Price paid~€600M (~$650M) total for 100% of Sauber Holding AG. This figure is described as "in line with earlier paddock whispers" — the initial 75% stake was valued at ~$600M, so the full price tracks proportionally. Exact breakdown not publicly disclosed.
What Audi acquired100% of Sauber Holding AG, which owns: Sauber Motorsport AG (F1 constructor, Concorde Agreement holder, Hinwil base), plus all related IP, infrastructure, staff contracts and existing commercial agreements.
Regulatory approvalVW Group and Audi AG supervisory boards both voted to approve the full takeover. Standard EU merger control review. No FIA/FOM approval required for ownership change (only the team's Concorde Agreement must be maintained).
Rausing's exit termsIslero Investments fully exits. Rausing is understood to have received job protection commitments for Hinwil staff — a condition he had demanded since the Andretti approach in 2021. Premium price vs. market (team was valued below $1B at initial deal; QIA's later investment implied $1.2B).
QIA deal (separate)Qatar Investment Authority acquired ~30% of Sauber Holding AG from Audi in November 2024, at a reported ~$360M. This was NOT part of the original acquisition — Audi sold down a stake post-completion to raise capital and bring in a strategic partner. Total post-QIA: Audi 70%, QIA 30%.

Strategic risk: price paid vs. performance

Audi paid a fair price for the asset — but the asset was a struggling, demoralised team with 0 points in 2024 and leadership instability. The €600M bought infrastructure and a racing licence, not a competitive team. The real investment is in transformation: new personnel, new facilities (Bicester), new power unit (Neuburg), and time. Target: championship contention by 2030 — meaning 4+ seasons of development spend on top of the acquisition price.

Sauber's ownership and identity has changed more than almost any other team in F1 history — understanding this context is essential to understanding why the Audi acquisition happened the way it did.
